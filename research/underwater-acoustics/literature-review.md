# Sound Speed Estimation From Argo Float Data: A Literature Review

## Status and Scope

This file is the canonical literature review for the `underwater-acoustics` topic. Its organizing question is narrow: **what does the existing literature say about deriving ocean sound-speed structure from Argo float observations, and where does that literature still leave a methodologically important gap?**

The review focuses on seven connected themes:

1. sound-speed equations and thermodynamic standards
2. Argo-derived sound-speed climatologies and regional variability studies
3. vertical interpolation choices and their consequences
4. uncertainty quantification from sensors through mapped products
5. acoustic propagation, tomography, and operational applications
6. machine-learning and statistical profile modeling
7. the expanding observational scope of the Argo program itself

The bibliography below continues to be normalized and linked to local source copies in [sources/](sources/), but the narrative is written as a source-backed standalone review rather than as a source-inventory note.

Review status note: most narrative citations have now been checked against local full text and carry page references where verified. A smaller set of active references still remains pending full-text review, so unpaged citations to those sources should be treated as provisional rather than fully verified.

## Overview

Argo float data now underpin a substantial fraction of the modern literature on large-scale ocean sound-speed estimation. The reviewed work ranges from climatological mapping and regional variability studies to propagation diagnostics, sound-channel characterization, machine-learning reconstruction, and operational forecasting applications. Across those literatures, one methodological asymmetry stands out. Equation choice, sensor quality control, mapped-product uncertainty, and downstream acoustic use are all discussed in some detail, but the **profile-level interpolation step between native Argo temperature-salinity observations and derived sound-speed structure is often left implicit**. That omission matters because adjacent literatures have already shown that interpolation choice can materially alter derived ocean-state quantities, and because the reviewed Argo-to-sound-speed studies are still much stronger on descriptive analysis than on explicit comparative validation of those processing choices. It matters especially because sound speed depends nonlinearly on temperature, salinity, and pressure, so interpolation error in the upstream state variables does not propagate into acoustically relevant diagnostics in a simple one-to-one way.

## Sound Speed Equations: From UNESCO to TEOS-10

The sound-speed-equation literature establishes a clear historical lineage. Wilson (1960, p. 641) is the early empirical baseline; Del Grosso (1974, pp. 1084-1085) and Chen and Millero (1977, pp. 1129-1130) superseded that earlier formulation; and Chen-Millero was then codified within the UNESCO / EOS-80 framework by Fofonoff and Millard (1983, pp. 3-4). The associated comparison literature, including Spiesberger and Metzger (1991), Dushaw et al. (1993, pp. 255-256), Spiesberger (1993, p. 2235), Millero and Li (1994, pp. 2757-2758), Wong and Zhu (1995, p. 1732), and Meinen and Watts (1997, pp. 2058-2059), shows that Del Grosso remained an important reference point and was often treated as competitive with, or preferable to, UNESCO-era formulations for deep-ocean acoustic work.

Alongside those higher-fidelity formulations, a simpler practical family remained in active use, including Mackenzie (1981, p. 807), Coppens (1981, p. 862), Leroy, Robinson, and Goldsmith (2008, pp. 2774-2775), Pike and Beiboer (1993), and Brewer et al. (2015, pp. 591-592). Taken together, these papers define an enduring tradeoff between computational simplicity, physical realism, and validity across pressure-salinity-temperature regimes.

The modern thermodynamic standard is TEOS-10, anchored in IOC, SCOR, and IAPSO (2010, pp. 2-3, 7), Feistel (2003, pp. 43-44), Feistel (2008, pp. 1639-1640), Feistel et al. (2010, pp. 633-634), Pawlowicz et al. (2012, pp. 161-162), McDougall et al. (2003, pp. 730-731), and Roquet et al. (2015, pp. 29-30). In this literature, TEOS-10 matters less as a new acoustic subfield than as a more internally consistent thermodynamic framework for seawater properties, with Roquet et al. (2015, pp. 29-30) supplying the computationally efficient implementation path used in practical toolchains. Allen et al. (2017, pp. 810-811) and Allen et al. (2025, pp. 834-835) extend that framework into sound-speed-instrument salinity calculations. The equation literature therefore provides a strong basis for separating equation-choice effects from the rest of the Argo-to-sound-speed workflow.

## Argo-Derived Sound Speed Climatologies and Regional Variability

Argo-enabled sound-speed studies now span global climatology, climate-change applications, basin-scale diagnostics, and sound-channel classification. At the global scale, Chu and Fan (2024, pp. 2-3) provide a global climatological dataset of acoustic parameters derived from WOA 2023, while Affatati, Scaini, and Salon (2022, pp. 2-3) and Possenti et al. (2023, pp. 2-4) extend the topic into climate-change impact assessment by linking warming and circulation change to sound-speed and ducting changes.

At the regional scale, Jana et al. (2022, pp. 2-4) are a central Argo-based example. They report that Bay of Bengal sound-speed variability peaks in the thermocline near 110 m, increases again toward the surface, is strongest near the surface in the northern bay and in the subsurface along the western boundary, and in the northern Bay of Bengal is associated with positive near-surface gradients that support strong surface ducting and a shadow zone below the sonic layer. Their workflow screens Argo temperature-salinity profiles, retains profiles spanning the upper ocean through at least 500 m, interpolates temperature, salinity, and sound speed to a 1 m vertical grid from 5 m to 500 m, and then analyzes sound-speed variability, sonic-layer-relevant gradients, surface ducting, and shadow-zone structure. But this is also where the reporting gap becomes visible. Jana et al. (2022, p. 3) state the target 1 m grid but do not specify the interpolation algorithm, whereas closely related Arabian Sea papers do: Udaya Bhaskar, Swain, and Ravichandran (2008, pp. 148-149) report linear interpolation to 1 m, and Udaya Bhaskar and Swain (2016, pp. 1265-1266) report Akima-spline interpolation to 1 m. Arabian Sea studies by Udaya Bhaskar et al. (2012, pp. 36-39) similarly tie upper-ocean structure to sonic-layer and mixed-layer behavior. A related moored-buoy study by Srinivasu et al. (2024, pp. 2-5) extends that regional acoustic picture in the southeastern Arabian Sea. Additional regional studies in the Northwest Pacific, Kuroshio Extension, Mediterranean, Banda Sea, and South China Sea further show that Argo-derived sound-speed analyses are already established as a regional ocean-acoustics practice rather than as a single isolated Bay of Bengal case (Zhang et al., 2023, pp. 619-620; Chen et al., 2017, pp. 3-4; Salon et al., 2003, pp. 833-834; Armansyah et al., 2018, pp. 62-63; Liu, Xintong, et al., 2024, pp. 2-3).

This regional literature also extends into sound-channel and classification problems. Prasanna Kumar et al. (1993, pp. 17-18), Hjelmervik et al. (2011, pp. 253-254), Hjelmervik and Hjelmervik (2013, pp. 809-810), Zhang et al. (2009, pp. 23-24), Mandelberg and Frizzell-Makowski (2000), and Liu and Qu (2023, pp. 2-3) all support the broader point that sound-channel structure and acoustically stable regimes are being inferred from ocean-state structure in regional and basin-scale acoustic studies. Penupothu (2025) extends that trajectory toward ML-oriented SSP datasets.

Taken together, these studies show that Argo-derived sound-speed work is already geographically diverse and acoustically consequential. But they are still predominantly descriptive studies of regional structure, climatology, and acoustic interpretation rather than comparative tests of alternative profile-processing workflows. The remaining question is not whether Argo can support sound-speed analysis, but which processing choices most strongly shape the resulting products and how directly those choices have been validated.

## Vertical Interpolation Methods Remain Under-Reported

Vertical interpolation is the clearest process-level weakness in the reviewed literature. Many Argo-to-sound-speed studies report that profiles were placed on standard depth levels, but they do not state the interpolation algorithm used. That omission is striking because adjacent literatures already show that interpolation is not a neutral implementation detail.

Reiniger and Ross (1968, pp. 185-188) remain the classical oceanographic reference for profile interpolation. Cheng and Zhu (2014, pp. 1383-1384, 1393-1395) demonstrate that both linear and spline interpolation can introduce systematic temperature biases in ocean heat-content estimation relative to Reiniger-Ross. Barker and McDougall (2020, pp. 606-607) provide the modern state of the art through MR-PCHIP and MRST-PCHIP, with direct relevance to Argo temperature-salinity profile interpolation and with practical implementations carried into GSW. Fritsch and Carlson (1980, pp. 238-239), Akima (1970, pp. 2-5), Katsura (2021, p. 3), Li et al. (2022, pp. 2, 4-5), and North and Livingstone (2013, pp. 213-214) reinforce the point that interpolation choice affects shape preservation, monotonicity, and derived-quantity bias.

Li et al. (2022, pp. 2, 4-5) are especially important because they do more than report a single sensitivity number. They compare simple linear interpolation against Reiniger-Ross, MR-PCHIP, and MRST-PCHIP style alternatives and show that the choice of vertical scheme changes the inferred long-term trend in upper-ocean heat content and thermosteric sea-level rise. In their analysis, linear interpolation underestimates those trends by roughly 14% relative to the more advanced methods, with the largest problems appearing where coarse vertical spacing forces the interpolation to span strong gradients. That comparison does not transfer mechanically to sound speed, but it does generalize at the methodological level: sound speed is also a nonlinear function of temperature, salinity, and pressure, and many acoustically relevant diagnostics depend on the precise location and curvature of vertical features such as thermoclines, sonic layers, and duct boundaries. If interpolation can move those structures enough to bias heat-content trends, it is methodologically implausible to treat SSP interpolation as an inconsequential preprocessing step.

Coordinate choice is part of the same underreported processing chain. Argo’s native sampling, quality control, and derived subsurface-velocity products are organized in pressure space: profiling floats drift at target park pressures near 1000 dbar, profile between target pressures, and are distributed as temperature-salinity-pressure observations with companion park-level velocity estimates (Wong et al., 2020, pp. 3, 18). Likewise, Roemmich and Gilson (2009, pp. 81-84) construct their climatology on 58 standard pressure levels from 0 to 2000 dbar, carry out fitting and objective analysis separately at each pressure level, and convert WOA depth coordinates onto those same pressure surfaces for comparison. UNESCO-era seawater algorithms also treat pressure-to-depth conversion as a distinct downstream utility rather than as the native interpolation coordinate itself (Fofonoff and Millard, 1983, pp. 9, 29-32). Read together, these sources support a practical baseline: for Argo-derived SSP workflows, pressure is the native vertical coordinate, while depth conversion is mainly a compatibility step for external products, climatology comparison, or presentation.

That does not make isopycnal coordinates irrelevant. Schmidtko, Johnson, and Lyman (2013, pp. 1658-1660) explicitly build an isopycnal/mixed-layer climatology because it can preserve density structure and reduce some smoothing biases, but they also have to treat the mixed layer separately and then merge the result back onto a regular pressure grid. The literature therefore points to a calibrated distinction rather than a universal rule: pressure-space interpolation is the default operational choice for profile-to-profile mapping on native Argo axes, whereas isopycnal coordinates become a specialized alternative when the analysis target is explicitly water-mass-following structure rather than direct prediction on reported pressure levels.

The mapped-product literature reaches the same conclusion indirectly. Roemmich and Gilson (2009, pp. 81-82), Gaillard et al. (2016, pp. 1305-1308), Good, Martin, and Rayner (2013, pp. 6704-6706), Schmidtko, Johnson, and Lyman (2013, pp. 1658-1660), Gouretski (2018, pp. 1127-1129), Zhang et al. (2022, pp. 2-4), and Zhou et al. (2023, pp. 2-3) all imply different vertical or mixed-layer handling choices. Wong et al. (2020, p. 8) are especially important here because Argo itself distributes native-level profiles without prescribing a single interpolation method. The literature therefore supports a precise gap statement: interpolation has demonstrable consequences for derived ocean quantities, but explicit per-profile interpolation comparisons for Argo-derived sound-speed structure remain rare, and competing profile-processing choices are only infrequently compared through direct validation rather than descriptive reconstruction alone.

The software-tooling layer reinforces that gap. Maze and Balem (2020, pp. 2-3) describe `argopy` as a Python package intended to simplify Argo data access and manipulation through region/float/profile fetch APIs, standard-versus-expert user modes, profile/measurement reshaping, and interpolation onto standard pressure levels. Read that way, `argopy` is important enabling infrastructure for Argo analysis, but it is still positioned around access, formatting, and practical manipulation rather than as a dedicated uncertainty-quantification framework. That leaves a visible gap between the more sophisticated interpolation and uncertainty literature and the day-to-day tooling used to manipulate Argo profiles.

## Uncertainty Quantification: Sensor Precision Versus Mapping Error

The uncertainty literature separates into four layers: sensor precision, delayed-mode quality control and correction, equation-side uncertainty, and mapped-product discrepancy.

At the sensor level, Wong et al. (2020, p. 2) and Hosoda et al. (2019, pp. 2-3) provide the practical accuracy baseline for Argo CTDs. These sources support temperature uncertainty on the order of a few thousandths of a degree Celsius, salinity uncertainty from a few thousandths to roughly one hundredth of a practical salinity unit depending on context, and pressure uncertainty on the order of a few decibars. The current Argo QC conventions also formalize automatic real-time profile tests and QC-flag policy, including impossible-date and impossible-location checks, global and regional range tests, pressure-monotonicity checks, spike tests, and density-inversion screening (Wong et al., 2025, pp. 10-15). Wong, Gilson, and Cabanes (2023, pp. 2-3), Ishikawa et al. (2024, pp. 2-3), Wong, Johnson, and Owens (2003, p. 2), Owens and Wong (2009, pp. 450-452), and Cabanes, Thierry, and Lagadec (2016, pp. 128-129) extend that picture into delayed-mode salinity drift, gray-listing, and the system-level impacts of QC choices.

Equation-side uncertainty also has a meaningful literature. Allen et al. (2017, pp. 810-811), Allen et al. (2025, pp. 834-836), and Grekov, Grekov, and Sychov (2021) show that sound-speed error propagation can be treated formally rather than heuristically. Taken together, those papers indicate that sensor-side and equation-side uncertainties can be carried into derived sound-speed uncertainty estimates.

The larger contrast in the literature is between low instrument-level uncertainty and much larger mapped-product spread. Zhou et al. (2023, pp. 2-3), Liu, Chao, et al. (2024, pp. 2-4), Good et al. (2013, pp. 6704-6706), and Kuusela and Stein (2018, pp. 20-22) together suggest that gridded temperature-salinity products can diverge enough to imply sound-speed discrepancies of several meters per second, even though formal uncertainty fields remain unevenly exposed across products. Roemmich and Gilson (2009, pp. 81-82) treat mapping and sampling error as a core methodological issue, but the distributed Roemmich-Gilson Argo Climatology product (Scripps Institution of Oceanography) does not expose user-facing uncertainty fields. By contrast, Good et al. (2013, pp. 6705-6706) explicitly publish uncertainty alongside the EN4 analyses, which provides a direct example of the kind of output the dominant Argo climatology does not provide. Kuusela and Stein (2018, pp. 20-22) then supply the methodological contrast by showing that calibrated uncertainty can be built directly into Argo interpolation rather than left implicit.

The downstream uncertainty literature extends beyond mapping into forecast propagation. Thacker et al. (2012, pp. 52-53) and Iskandarani et al. (2016, pp. 4819-4820) show that once Argo-informed fields enter operational forecast systems, uncertainty can be propagated formally at the simulation layer through polynomial expansions and related UQ methods. Those studies are not acoustics papers, but they matter because they show that mapped-field uncertainty is only one stage in a longer downstream chain. Within that chain, profile interpolation remains an underintegrated source of uncertainty rather than merely an upstream implementation detail.

## Acoustic Applications Increasingly Depend on Argo

The reviewed literature shows that Argo-derived sound-speed information is already tightly coupled to both research and operational acoustics. Operational forecasting and naval oceanography are represented by Chassignet et al. (2009, pp. 64-65), Cummings (2005, pp. 3583-3584), Cummings and Smedstad (2013, pp. 303-305), Le Traon et al. (2019, pp. 2-3), Lellouche et al. (2018, pp. 1093-1095), Lellouche et al. (2021, pp. 2-3), Tonani et al. (2015, pp. s201-s203), and Chassignet et al. (2020, pp. 4595-4597). Read together, these sources show that operational systems assimilate in situ temperature-salinity observations, including Argo profiles, into three-dimensional forecast fields used well beyond descriptive climatology.

The acoustic relevance of those fields is documented more directly by Chu, Haeger, et al. (2002, pp. 563-564), which treats hydrographic data errors as a source of acoustic uncertainty. The operational oceanography literature and the acoustic-uncertainty literature therefore intersect cleanly: Argo-informed analyses and forecasts are upstream inputs to acoustically relevant environmental fields, even when the acoustic model is not the primary focus of the oceanographic papers.

Liu et al. (2025, pp. 6-10, 19-20) provide a concrete downstream example in GNSS-A seafloor positioning. Their workflow compares in situ sound-speed profiles against profiles derived from the HYCOM/NCODA global ocean analysis system, whose assimilated observations include XBT, CTD, glider, and Argo profile data. Relative to the in situ profiles, HYCOM-derived profiles change GNSS-A positions by 0.2 cm RMS horizontally and 2.9 cm vertically, whereas a fixed Munk profile produces 0.6 cm and 10.3 cm RMS differences, respectively. They conclude that assimilative global-ocean-analysis profiles can act as a cost-effective substitute for many practical GNSS-A operations, especially unmanned-surface-vehicle campaigns where full-depth in situ SSP measurement is difficult.

Ocean acoustic tomography provides a particularly revealing comparison system. Dushaw (2019, pp. 183-185), Dushaw (2022, pp. 34-35), Woolfe et al. (2015, pp. 2878-2879), and Munk, Worcester, and Wunsch (1995) show that Argo and tomography constrain related but incomplete aspects of the same thermal structure, and that line-integrated acoustic constraints can reveal limits in profile-based observing systems. Sensitivity and propagation-focused studies by Chen et al. (2017, pp. 3-4), Chen et al. (2018, pp. 26-27), and Xu et al. (2024, pp. 2-3) further show that Argo-derived SSP variability is already being treated as acoustically consequential in specific propagation settings. Nystuen et al. (2011) and Delaigue et al. (2026, pp. 101-102) extend the connection into more direct bridges between profiling floats and passive-acoustic observation systems.

The remaining gap in this literature is therefore not whether sound speed matters to acoustics. It is how processing choices and uncertainty enter the Argo-derived sound-speed products that acoustics workflows already depend on.

## Machine Learning and Statistical Methods Advance Rapidly

The literature on Argo-derived prediction and interpolation is moving rapidly toward probabilistic and machine-learning approaches. On the statistical side, Kuusela and Stein (2018, pp. 20-22), Liu and Cao (2025, pp. 2-4), and Chen et al. (2023, pp. 2-3) show that Gaussian-process-based and related methods already provide a strong uncertainty language for nonstationary, heteroskedastic ocean fields.

The neural-network literature points in a similar direction. Sauzède et al. (2017, pp. 1-2) introduce CANYON; Bittig et al. (2018, pp. 1-2) extend that line with CANYON-B and explicit local uncertainty estimates; Carter et al. (2021, pp. 785-786) generalize the approach through the ESPER routines; Fourrier et al. (2020, pp. 1-2) regionalize it for the Mediterranean through CANYON-MED; and Osborne et al. (2024, pp. 1-2) use GOM-NNpH as a secondary QC and sensor-assessment tool for Gulf of Mexico BGC-Argo pH profiles. Together with Pietropolli et al. (2024, pp. 7347-7349) and Amadio et al. (2024, pp. 689-691), these papers show that local, input-dependent uncertainty for Argo-derived variables is already established elsewhere in the Argo ecosystem. The broader sound-speed ML literature is now extensive, spanning Madiligama, Zou, and Zhang (2025, pp. 2-3), Liu, Yu, et al. (2024, pp. 2-4), Li and Zhai (2022, pp. 572-573), Huang et al. (2021, pp. 488-489), and Ahmed, Younis, and De Leon (2021, pp. 436-437). These studies show that predictive SSP modeling is advancing quickly, but they do not eliminate the need to understand uncertainty introduced earlier in the profile-processing chain. In most cases they inherit already gridded, reconstructed, or standardized profile inputs, which means the upstream interpolation choice is absorbed into the training target rather than removed from the workflow. A high-performing model can therefore reproduce interpolation-conditioned structure at scale without telling the user how much of that structure reflects the ocean and how much reflects preprocessing.

Broader spatiotemporal interpolation work matters here mainly for what it shows about expected methodological standards. Troupin et al. (2012, pp. 90-91) and Barth et al. (2014, pp. 225-227) make uncertainty-bearing interpolation a first-class product rather than an undocumented preprocessing step, while Oke et al. (2021, pp. 2-3) and Lyman and Johnson (2023, pp. 575-576) show the same expectation carrying into modern Argo-satellite fusion and regression-based ocean-state mapping. The point is not that these papers solve the SSP problem directly. It is that adjacent Argo applications increasingly expose interpolation structure and uncertainty explicitly, whereas much of the Argo-to-sound-speed literature still absorbs those choices into the background.

## The Argo Program and Its Expanding Observational Frontier

The broader Argo literature, centered on Johnson et al. (2022, pp. 379-381), Riser et al. (2016, pp. 145-147), Roemmich and the Argo Steering Team (2009, pp. 46-47), Roemmich et al. (2019, p. 2), Jayne et al. (2017, pp. 18-20), and Thierry et al. (2025, pp. 2-3), shows that Argo has already evolved from a core temperature-salinity profiling array into a multi-mission observing system with deep and biogeochemical extensions.

That expansion matters directly for acoustics. Zilberman et al. (2023, pp. 2-3) show that Deep Argo extends temperature-salinity observations below 2000 m and reduces uncertainty in characterizing the full ocean volume, while Iqbal et al. (2019) suggests the direct relevance of deeper-profile coverage for sound-speed estimation. Taken together, these sources suggest that deeper-profile coverage is likely to reduce the need to extrapolate shallower profiles into deeper acoustic regimes, even if that acoustic implication is still more often inferred than tested directly in the reviewed literature. Pan et al. (2026, pp. 2-3), von Schuckmann and Le Traon (2011, pp. 783-784), and Lynch et al. (2018, pp. 174-176) link changing heat content and stratification to changing acoustic environments over time. The observing-system trajectory therefore increases, rather than decreases, the practical stakes of profile-level sound-speed processing decisions.

## Conclusion: The Remaining Methodological Gap

The reviewed literature converges on a specific methodological gap. Equation choice is mature; Argo-derived acoustic climatologies and regional variability studies are established; operational and research acoustics already depend on Argo-informed sound-speed structure; and adjacent statistical and machine-learning work already treats uncertainty as a first-class problem. What remains weakly documented, weakly quantified, and only rarely examined through direct comparative validation is the **profile-level vertical interpolation step between raw Argo temperature-salinity observations and derived sound-speed structure**. Because sound speed is a nonlinear transformation of temperature, salinity, and pressure, the most defensible unresolved question is not whether Argo matters for acoustics, but how much downstream variability in sound speed and acoustically relevant diagnostics is attributable not to the seawater equation or the observing platform alone, but to profile-processing decisions that have often been left implicit in published workflows. That comparison is most meaningfully framed in sound-speed space rather than inferred only from upstream temperature and salinity differences.
